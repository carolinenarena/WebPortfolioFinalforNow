Oklahoma State Court: Rape Defined

            In many states, there are no legal consequences for the act of marital rape.  Looking specifically at Oklahoma’s state law defining rape, its discourse allows people guilty of committing marital rape to stay out of the court room. The following passage is the active law used and analyzed for all cases of potential rape convictions.  Because this law is still used today, my revisions are even more so needed of attention.  The diction used in this law as well as some other state’s rape defining laws are unacceptable. Similar laws can be found in states such as North Carolina. Besides the point, many states and other countries are able to have active laws that does not recognize that consent is still required in marital sex.  In the following passage, I want to highlight the dismissal of marital rape, the inadmissible support of a gender binary, and a few other issues included in Oklahoma’s current law defining rape:

A. Rape is an act of sexual intercourse involving vaginal or anal penetration accomplished with a male or female who is not the spouse of the perpetrator and who may be of the same or the opposite sex as the perpetrator under any of the following circumstances:

1. Where the victim is under sixteen (16) years of age;

2. Where the victim is incapable through mental illness or any other unsoundness of mind, whether temporary or permanent, of giving legal consent;

3. Where force or violence is used or threatened, accompanied by apparent power of execution to the victim or to another person;

4. Where the victim is intoxicated by a narcotic or anesthetic agent, administered by or with the privity of the accused as a means of forcing the victim to submit;

5. Where the victim is at the time unconscious of the nature of the act and this fact is known to the accused;

6. Where the victim submits to sexual intercourse under the belief that the person committing the act is a spouse, and this belief is induced by artifice, pretense, or concealment practiced by the accused or by the accused in collusion with the spouse with intent to induce that belief. In all cases of collusion between the accused and the spouse to accomplish such act, both the spouse and the accused, upon conviction, shall be deemed guilty of rape;

…

B. Rape is an act of sexual intercourse accomplished with a male or female who is the spouse of the perpetrator if force or violence is used or threatened, accompanied by apparent power of execution to the victim or to another person.

 

            Specifically looking at clause A of the Oklahoma State Law, Section 1111- rape Defined, they state rape as an act of “vaginal or anal penetration”.  Only mentioning vaginal or anal penetration is restrictive and keeps the court from being able to prosecute anyone who has forced oral sex or any other sexual act on a person regardless of penetration.  Is this law saying forced oral sex within a marriage or not is without consequence? Is this law saying violence and sexual intimacy without consent is legal as long as penetration does not occur? It is important to acknowledge that there is a multitude of situations better fit the true definition of rape.  Ultimately, the severity is only experienced by the victim.  For that reason, I think it is completely unreasonable to determine rape by penetration.  The trauma and fear a rape victim goes­­ through are still present whether anal or vaginal penetration has occurred.  I would change this clause to be more inclusive of all sexual acts that have occurred without consent between all partners. 

            The clause continues by stating that penetration must be “accomplished with a male or female”.  The law excludes all intersex and trans bodies, as well as anyone that doesn’t identify with any gender.  All of these people excluded are capable of being victims or perpetrators of rape.  The diction of only “male or female” is supporting our country’s gender binary.  The support of a gender binary is present through discourse in the media, books, shows, articles, etc. Not only is the gender binary enforced as a social construct but here it is reinforced in the court room.  Because of the terminology “male or female”, for example, an intersex person is capable of committing or being a victim of rape without consequences. Later in this phrase, it states rape is between “same or opposite sex”.  The law assumes there are two opposite genders, and further supports the gender binary.  There is a diversity of gender identities, and none of them are so called “opposites” of each other.  Because of the reinforced gender binary, the law excludes intersex, trans, and people who don’t identify with any gender.

            The last phrase in Clause A that I want to highlight is “who is not the spouse of the perpetrator”.  The word “spouse” refers to the person married to the perpetrator.  Clause A dismisses the existence of marital rape. Because of clause A, rape with or without the use of violence, drugs, etc. is completely legal between partners who are married, even when consent is not given by all partners.  Not only does it completely disregard marital rape when both partners are sober, but as the law continues and explains that when “the victim is intoxicated by a narcotic or anesthetic agent”, only if it is not the victim’s spouse. Regardless, the diction used neglects the bottom line that sober and active consent must be given each and every time despite the status of any relationship.

            Later we see lawmakers added Clause B that states that rape can occur between spouses in a marriage if “force of violence is used or threatened”.  Although this is an improvement by recognizing marital rape exists, unfortunately, it is only recognized as rape if violence is used.  This does not include the use of drugs, forcing sex onto a spouse while unconscious or asleep, or even just sex without active consent.  Rape does not have to occur with violence.  There are many instances where violence is not used. Why should it be any different for a couple who is married?  Sex should still be consensual.  There is no vow or marriage law that states that sex should no longer need to be consensual.  No matter the relationship, sex without active consent, should be tried in court and recognized as rape.  It should be noted that I specifically use “active consent”.  Active consent includes the right of a partner to take back consent at any point during sexual intercourse.

            With all considerations, a revised and thoughtful version of the Oklahoma State Law in defining rape follows:

A. Rape is any act of sexual intercourse or intimacy accomplished between people in any type of relationship, including none at all and who may be of the same or different sex as the perpetrator under any of the following circumstances:

1. Where the victim is under sixteen (16) years of age;

2. Where the victim is incapable through mental illness or any other unsoundness of mind, whether temporary or permanent, of giving legal consent;

3. Where force or violence is used or threatened, accompanied by apparent power of execution to the victim or to another person;

4. Where the victim is intoxicated by a narcotic or anesthetic agent, administered by or with the privity of the accused as a means of forcing the victim to submit;

5. Where the victim is at the time unconscious of the nature of the act and this fact is known to the accused;

6. Where the victim gives consent to sexual intercourse under the belief that the person committing the act is a spouse, and this belief is induced by artifice, pretense, or concealment practiced by the accused or by the accused in collusion with the spouse with intent to induce that belief. In all cases of collusion between the accused and the spouse to accomplish such act, both the spouse and the accused, upon conviction, shall be deemed guilty of rape;

7. Where the victim withdrawals consent during sexual intimacy, but the perpetrator continues without active consent;

…

B. Rape is any act of sexual intercourse or sexual intimacy accomplished if force or violence is used or threatened, accompanied by apparent power of execution to the victim or to another person.

 

            The main goal of the revision was to include all genders, all situations, and all relationships in being potential victims or perpetrators of rape.  It is easy to unintentionally leave out certain groups or situations, especially in 1981 when the law was written. The diction we use today, is very different from the diction used in 1981.  Intersex bodies, trans people, people who don’t identify with a gender, marital rape, active consent, etc. are all people and situations we are more conscious of including today.  Although the word choice of Oklahoma’s definition of rape might seem acceptable in 1981, the fact that this exact law is still used in courtroom’s today is unacceptable. 

The lawmakers in Oklahoma blatantly exclude many people and situations in the active version of the law above.  It is so important that we recognize and accept all genders, all relationships, and all situations.  The previous analysis is not of an article, or a movie, it is of a law that is used to decide whether someone is convicted of rape or not.  Judges analyze and use each and every word to consider the outcome of a trial.  Because of the exclusion of many genders and situations, people’s lives are greatly affected.  Rapists are able to get off free through loopholes in Oklahoma’s law.  The revisions I made such as between people in any type of relationship, including none at all and who may be of the same or different sex made the diction vaguer, but now is able to include everyone and allow them to get the justice or repercussions.  As people, we have to start paying greater attention to each word we use, in particular in the diction of active laws, because it is inappropriate and inadmissible to continue to exclude people from the same rights as others. 

The vagueness that was added has benefitted the law in this situation.  A new law supported by the Prime Minister of Sweden has arose following the rise of the # MeToo Movement in the US.  Sweden has moved to define rape simply as “sex without consent”.  The idea of a vaguer definition for rape was brought to attention by a small political group in Sweden called FATTA.  In the sixth clause I used wording like Sweden’s law in attempt to include all situations of rape.  I also added a seventh clause to specify that consent needs to be active, meaning that consent can be taken back at any point of sexual intimacy.  If consent is taken back and perpetrator continues, this would also be considered rape. Being unclear with word choice does not always mean a more inclusive law.  It is important to determine the meaning of laws in specific situations.  However, it is practically impossible to include every rape situation out there.  For that reason, vaguer word choices are beneficial in defining rape in the practice of law.  This approach to law using ambiguous diction could be the next step in improving our country’s ability to convict more cases of rape that occur.
