Polemic: Transgender People Participating in Athletics

Attack

It should be stated that all transgender people should be treated equally and given the same opportunities as any other human.  However, with the new conflict of transgender women in sports, it is difficult to debate whether cisgender women are at a biological disadvantage to transgender women pre full hormone transition.  Transgender women shouldn’t be excluded from sports, but hormone levels and a few other biologically advantageous factors should determine which category of sports they participate in.  If they have the hormone levels of a biological male, they should participate in men’s sports.   For these reasons, I believe that individuals should participate with their biological sex at birth. It is unarguable that women and men perform at different levels in sports, and those advantages don’t go away post transition.  Looking at professional records, for example the mile record holder.  For men, the world record is 3:43, while the female’s world record is 4:12.  It is evident in this situation and many other situations that men are able to outperform women.  On average, women perform at a 10-12% lower ability than men.  There are many factors that contribute to this including societal conditioning, testosterone, etc.  Societal conditioning is a factor that is tied in with biological abilities:  although it is not good to encourage males any more than females to participate in sports, societal conditioning could possibly stem from the biological factors that men obtain to perform at higher levels.  In addition, although not true for all women, many women tend to have wider hips, smaller lungs, and other biological factors that should be considered in comparing performances of women vs men in physical activity.  Testosterone is commonly brought up and supports male’s physical advantages because it does increase strength and aerobic capacity.  Men, on average, have higher testosterone levels than women, and create an advantage in activities like sports.  Pictured in this image is Caster Semenya, a cisgender female who was not allowed to participate in the Olympics due to her advantageous testosterone levels Because of this advantage, many cisgender women in high school have a decreased chance in receiving scholarships from colleges.  They have worked hard their whole lives, and many pre transition transgender women that have started participating in women’s sports have taken many of these scholarship offers.  Many of these girls start practicing and devoting much of their time to a sport they are passionate about.  They spend hours and hours perfecting their skills and training for a sport, only to be disappointed when someone with a biological advantage takes their spot.  This could also go for college athletes looking to participate in professional sports as a career as well.  A main argument for creating a fair environment for all genders comes from the idea of the skill thesis.  The skill thesis states that a person’s success should come from skill and skill only.  This means that there is no unequal advantages or unequal starting points in any type of competition.  This could support why we separated sports into men’s and women’s sports originally.  We accept that there are genetic differences such as hormone levels that can affect how one performs in sports or any physical activity through accepting the gender separation that already exists.  This occurred so that we would be able to celebrate, and athletes would be able to strive for success within their own gender.  If we didn’t separate sports into these categories, many records overall are maintained by men.  Women would be greatly unrepresented in many sports.   This is true for professional sports, but also is true for high school levels sports.  Personally, I have experienced seeing this divide in my high school career.  The best female 5K times would be around 19:00, while male 5K times could be as fast as 15:30.  This was not just my school either, going to meets all around the state, you can gather that there is significant performance differences between males and females in specifically cross country.  Cross country isn’t the only sport that you notice this divide.  To further support the skill thesis, we see sports like wrestling level out the playing field by performing in weight categories.  To counter the argument that many sports don’t follow the skill thesis, I would argue that actually some do.  Sports like wrestling recognize an unfair advantage to factor like weight.  They level out the starting point in order to truly determine success that depends on pure skill and hard work.  To support the skill thesis, athletes should participate in the category of their biological sex.

Defense

After bringing up this topic to many of friends and family, I have heard one argument a few times that has stuck with me.  It is this idea that men can just “choose” to become a woman and use their biological advantages to gain success over cisgender women.  This argument has stuck with me because many people arguing to exclude transgender women from sports don’t understand that many of these women transitioning are going through so much, both emotionally and physically if they are using hormone or surgeries to transition.  The fact that people think cisgender men are choosing to become women frustrates me.  Before going into further arguments, I want to preface by asking you to put yourself in a transgender women’s shoes.  Imagine, or relate if you have experienced these feelings, but imagine that every day you feel that you are the wrong gender.  You are suppressed into the gender constructs and taught to behave a certain way that fits into your biological sex.  But what if you don’t feel like yourself in those constructs.  What if you feel like you fit into another sex’s construct, or what if you feel like you fit into both, or neither.  Transgender women are not men who decide to become a woman.  Transgender women are women and transgender men are men.  Although I am going to discuss mainly transgender women, these beliefs and arguments are intended to support the equality of all genders.  My whole point is to bring attention and sympathize with transgender people as they are already facing a country who still has people advocating against the rights of transgender people.  In my opinion, an unarguable point supporting the participation of transgender people in all sports is Title IX.  Title IX states “[n]o person in the United States may, upon the basis of sex, be excluded from participation in, be denied the benefits of, or be subjected to discrimination under any education program or activity receiving federal financial assistance”.    Regardless of your support of transgender athletes, this law to end sex discrimination in schools was created in 1972, and should still be followed and enforced in every aspect.  Although this covers only high school and college athletes due to the wording “in schools”, I think it should be enforced in professional sports as well.  Professional sports are careers, and it is immoral to keep an individual from a job based on their gender identity. 

To counteract the much-discussed testosterone argument, we have to recognize there are so many factors that play a part in the success of one athlete to another.  According to Sara Chodosh, a graduate of the University of Pennsylvania with a degree in neuroscience and philosophy of science, there is no true correlation of testosterone levels and performance levels.  She explains that there has been no conclusive evidence of testosterone and its effects on women’s athletic performance.  There are many factors ranging from societal conditioning to a variety of hormones that can affect one’s athletic performance.  In order to study testosterone, you have to look at natural hormone levels and compare them to performance.  It is true that on average, cisgender men have higher levels of testosterone levels than cisgender women.  However, if we use that data, then we would also be able to conclude that on average, cisgender men with higher testosterone levels would perform better than cisgender men who have a lower level of testosterone, and that is not true. If testosterone truly did affect athletic performance, we would be able to predict athletic performance of someone based on their hormone levels.  There is not enough reliable and clear evidence correlating performance with testosterone, so there should be no argument that transgender women have an advantage over cisgender women due to testosterone levels.  

  As an alternative solution to having the transgender individuals participate in athletics, we could get rid of “womens” and “mens” sports as categories.  The categories could be determined regardless of gender similar to wrestling where height, and weight, biological advantages, are considered.  It is possible to create categories depending on height, weight, and also a variety of hormone levels.  This would also support an environment that accepted gender as fluid, allowing people of all genders to participate in athletics while deconstructing the gender binary at the same time.

Metacognition

When choosing to discuss transgender individual’s rights in athletics, I hadn’t realized all of the complicated and conflicting arguments I had to consider. While writing the attack, I found it really hard to find the words to explain and convey an opinion that I didn’t truly believe.  I often would run out of ideas and found it much harder to get into “a flow” of writing for the attack compared with the defense.  In addition, after writing the attack, I had to rethink all of my arguments for my defense.  While writing my defense I often found myself wording arguments best so someone from the other side couldn’t take it down.  Writing the attack first definitely strengthened my ideas and how I conveyed them in my defense.  Ultimately, I  knew that I wanted to support transgender’s rights to participate in the gender that they identified with.  However, it was really hard for me to get past the evidence there is supporting differences between male and female performances.  This was really hard for me to get past because I was an athlete myself in high school.  Especially in a sport like cross country, I can say from experience that I had trained harder and longer than some of the men on the team.  However, my results didn’t always show for that.  Many of the guys who didn’t care or didn’t train as hard as I did still had race times similar if not better than mine.  I was frustrated, but in the end their race time didn’t affect how well I ranked.  It was really difficult to argue against this point, and it was the only point I could relate to from the opposing side. I realized that the issue couldn’t be solved by making laws that allowed transgender women and men to participate in sports. These laws would just further support the gender binary that we have in our country.  I think that having several categories to represent the gender spectrum that we are pushing towards will more likely help everyone as a whole.
